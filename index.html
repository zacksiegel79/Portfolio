AI In the Classroom

How AI shapes student learning, thinking, and well-being

Zack Siegel | Interactive News

AI isn’t as novel as it once was. It has become a background tool, like spellcheck or Google, quietly shaping how students read, write, study, and think. Whether it’s ChatGPT for brainstorming, Perplexity for research, or Grammarly’s new AI refocus for editing, many students are using AI tools daily, often without clear guidance on what “healthy” use actually looks like.

We will explore AI as a cognitive variable and question how outsourcing parts of thinking can change memory, critical thinking, motivation, and stress. Instead of treating AI as a moral controversy or a secret shortcut, it is more productive to map the real tradeoffs students are navigating in an engaging, easily accessible way, so readers can make more informed choices about the tools they already rely on.

The normalization of AI

Faculty Reports of Student Use of AI for Classroom-related Activities

Graphic from College Board’s AI research brief, “College Faculty Perceptions of Generative Artificial Intelligence in Higher Education”

AI use has moved from an emerging technology to everyday study tool for many students. College Board research summarizing prior briefs reports that a vast majority of high school students (84%) say they use generative AI tools for schoolwork, a sign that AI assistance is already normalized well before students reach college. In higher education, the same brief notes that daily or weekly generative AI use among U.S. college students rose from 14% in spring 2023 to 42% in spring 2025, suggesting adoption is accelerating fast on campuses.

Among younger students, Pew Research Center similarly finds AI chatbots are mainstream: 64% of U.S. teens (13–17) say they use AI chatbots, including about three-in-ten who use them daily. The largest use case? 57% say they’ve used chatbots to search for information and 54% say they’ve used them to get help with schoolwork.

Cognitive effects

What happens when thinking is outsourced?

In College Board’s faculty survey, large majorities agreed that AI tools make students less likely to develop critical thinking abilities, reduce students’ ability to express original ideas, and make them less likely to engage deeply with course material.



The same College Board brief reports widespread faculty agreement that AI tools can make students overly dependent on technology for basic tasks, raising concerns that routine cognitive work gets outsourced instead of practiced.



The OECD warns that successfully completing tasks with generative AI does not automatically produce learning benefits, and that offloading cognitive work to chatbots can create metacognitive laziness and disengagement, which may deter skill development long-term.



OECD summarizes studies where students produce higher-quality outputs with generative AI, but the advantage can disappear or even reverse on exams when access is removed, suggesting shallow understanding or reduced independent problem-solving practice.



OECD also notes that when AI is used with clear pedagogical intent (or paired with redesigned teaching strategies), it can improve learning and help foster skills like critical thinking, creativity, collaboration, and stronger argumentation in structured learning environments.



Mental health & stress

AI tools can affect student mental health in two directions at once. In a 2025 mini-review of experimental/empirical higher-ed studies, researchers note that AI can reduce stress when it supports learning in a personalized context (e.g., pacing and targeted help), and some students perceive AI as potentially beneficial for well-being.

At the same time, the review flags a number of risks that show up as AI becomes embedded in daily academic life: digital fatigue from increased screen time, technostress, and reduced face-to-face interaction, which can contribute to loneliness. It also warns that overreliance on AI may erode interpersonal skills and emotional intelligence, potentially increasing social isolation and anxiety, especially if AI starts replacing human support rather than supplementing it. The authors emphasize that evidence is still limited and argue for balanced AI integration that protects student well-being as adoption grows.

How students actually use AI

Faculty use of AI

Faculty perspectives are increasingly split between adopting AI as a useful and emerging educational tool, and deep concern for its unintended effects. In College Board’s higher-ed faculty survey, many instructors say AI has real upside as a support tool (personalized explanations, revision help), but the dominant focus is caution. Faculty widely worry that student AI use can undermine critical thinking and originality and make shallow work easier to submit as “real” learning. At the systems level, the OECD similarly frames generative AI as something that can improve teaching and learning when guided by pedagogy, but warns that unguided use can boost performance without real learning, and that academic integrity is a major pressure point for schools.

Only 23% of faculty report never using AI in their faculty role (meaning ~77% have used it).



37% of lower-secondary teachers used AI for their job in 2024.

92% are concerned about plagiarism/dishonesty; 88% about overreliance on automation; 79% about bias in outputs.
